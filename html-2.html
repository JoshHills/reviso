HTML: Closer Look

Time to get our hands dirty! This is where it gets exciting- writing your first website is the web-developer's equivalent of the programmer's 'hello world'- by the end of this course, you should be aiming to be both (you talented individual, you)! Correct structure is incredibly important, as what you provide is what's going to be processed, and you want to ensure that it's processed correctlyunless you'd class a broken website as 'art', or something weird like that. The rulesets we just talked about aren't just hearsay suggestions- the W3C actually publishes a 'Document Type Definition' for each release of HTML, which defines exactly what is and isn't right. Let's explore the right...

For posterity, these DTDs should be at least glanced at, but they're becoming almost flauntedhtml5 has actually been designed so that writing a dtd for it is impossible- due to certain self-defined elements, it would have to be technically infinite in this future of ours. Disregarding their technicalities, each HTML document should still include certain things...

What do I need?

There are three important things:

  • A line containing language information (what language, which versionin html5, this is much more simple- <!DOCTYPE html>).
  • A declarative header section that provides meta information about the document- this serves as a precursor to...
  • A body of content!

The second two should be wrapped in an <html> tag.

You can see this displayed in the example document below. You can write HTML in any text editor as long as you save the file with correct file extension.html, however you could also use something useful like an IDEindependent development environment - a text-editor on steroids; whatever you decide to use, it'll just be a matter of productivity. The Bare Necessities:

We touched brielfy on tags, classes and attributes. Kudos for remembering. Well, did you know there are two kinds of tags? 'Block-level' tagssuch as <div> and 'inline' tagssuch as <br />. The difference is that block-level tags construct the overall shape of the document, and inline tags, which can be nested inside, more specifically focus the content at a minute level. Let's look at the most useful examples!

Block Level

Headings: <h1> <h2> <h3> <h4> <h5> <h6>

Headings are a very important component in a page because they break up the content into chunks- they allow for quick scanning, and are also considerate for those who use screen readers. They're useful to pivot off, and as we'll see later, are useful in literal page navigation.

Paragraphs and Breaks: <p> <br /> <hr />

Paragraphs and breaks are the most important containers in a web-page, as they will server to shepherd and seperate the bulk of your website's content.

Lists: <ul> <ol> <li> <dl> <dt> <dd>

Lists are important aspects of any document- to string points together, or simply provide many at the same time. Three types of lists are supported by HTML - 'unordered lists' which display with bullet points, 'ordered lists' which display with numbers (both of which use 'list items' for their individual points), and 'definition lists', which link definition titles to their descriptions. Inline

These logical tags often supply semantic meaning by telling the client to render the element uniquely- they allow for a consistent visual disparity between content, whether it be for emphasis, or to provide additional functionality such as embedding hyperlinks. To use them, simply wrap the text you want to delineate around them.

Logical: <em> <strong> <code> <cite>

Physical: <b> <u> <s> <i>

These 'physical' tags are outdated, however are still used online- usually in comment-boxes that would like to permit you some minimal styling, but wont let you fiddle with their stylesheets.

Links:

Links at their basic are pretty easy to explain, but there's some interesting extra properties you can tweak that warrant inclusion! Defined as a connection from one web resource to another, links are vital in allowing the user to navigate your content, and also pointing to external resouces to make your website less enclosed- if somebody's took the time to compile an amazing foundry of information, you mightn't want to re-invent it for yourself in most instances.

As George Churchprofessor, harvard university; director in the personal genome project detailed, "the internet is amazing... for its connections. Going from footnotes to hyptertext to search-engines dramatically opens doors for evidence-based thinking, modeling, and collaboration". Concisely, openness is good stuff.

A fully fledged link comes under the guise of the 'anchor' tag, and might look <a href="html-2.html#my-link" target="_self" id="my-link" >like this<a>, which would be displayed graphically like this!

You may have noticed above that the HREF'hypermedia reference' attribute points towards an element on this pages, using the fragmentation pattern '#' to pin-point IDs. Well done for noticing that! The browser creates the full URI for you, so you can provide relative filepaths to local resources- by taking advantages of the identifying attribute like this you improve your website a tad.

Tables, we're going to have to have a talk...

Tables, and their associated tags<table> <tr> <td>..., are a bit of a cause of contempt for many web-developers. There was a time when the internet's sole structural aesthetic was comprised of tables, but with the advent of cascading stylesheets, they've taken a secondary seat. The reason for this, and subsequently my reasoning for bothering to explain it, is that tables signify a key lesson in web-design; they're a perfect example of what people tend to do wrong.

There exists a useful variety of HTML tags.
Some of them are vital, and some encourage extra features.

Using tables to provide layout for your content is generally frowned upon. This is because CSS wasn't meant to provide semantic-markup, rather, a lick of paint (as you'll see in a following topic). Tables are for tabular data- ergo, a specific purpose. As such, the only things you should really put in them are things such as statistics, or database-esque elements.

Although small amounts of non-semantic markup for layout purposes are fine, relying upon this sort of ethos indefinitely or heavily will cause you inveitable headaches in the future.

Enhancements can be added, however, to make your tabular data more pleasing to the eye- attributes such as border, width, cellspacing and cellpadding can be employed with ease to make your tables look as pretty as the key-points box above!
