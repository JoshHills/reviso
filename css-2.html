CSS: Closer Look

In this section, having covered the technical trickery of CSS, we’ll delve into some specific aspects that allow you to take full advantage of its features. The topics you’d likely most favour in this regard would be colour and size, as they allow you to finally inject some personality into your pages- with basic colour and sizing even the most anti-artisanal individual can produce pleasing spreads that are easy on the ol’ eyeballs! Also recieving an honourable mentino are pseudo-elements, which provide selectors for specially targeted parts of your DOM to perform stylings upon. You dig?

Know your limits!

Now that we can make things look graphically gorgeous, how do we go about sizing them? Well, CSS defines two surprisingly extensive categories of sizing units: relative and absolute. When setting any size, the length you apply will be a number followed by a unit’s abbreviationwithout any spaces- if you’re setting it to zero you may omit the unit for brevity.

When dealing with units, it’s important to not go overboard. As Marcos Ojeda, a designer at 'Khan Academy' documents: “Funny story: around 2010, I was working on a band's website and made the assumption [that units were infinite], so I left-positioned a div at -10,000,000px. About a week after launch, I got an email forwarded to me from an 'Opera' dev and fan of the group who was writing to let me know that ten million was too much left for the element and that their internal CSS parser just threw up its hands at very big values like that, breaking my layout.”

Tricky stuff, huh? He states that his trick worked at '-100,000px', and that the experience made him "forever skeptical of any css parser dealing with large numbers".

Absolutewhich aren’t recommended for use on-screen thanks to variation in size length units come as cm, mm, in, pxpixels, which are relative to the viewing device, meaning they may actually span more than one physical ‘pixel’, pt, and pc.

Relativewhich are great for scaling as they are specified relative to another length property length units come as emrelative to the font-size of the element, remrelative to the font-size of the root element, vw/vhrelative to 1% of the viewport, and the standard %. Em units multiple upon themselves when applied to font-size, so if an element with font size 1.2em was nested within an element with the same rule, the inner-element would have larger text- therefore, relative units aren’t impervious to quirks and quandaries.
Fix: use a good mix of both where appropriate.

Pseudo Elements:

Shh! They'll hear you!

Pseudo-elements are a rather large topic, and context specific. Instead of filling your faucets with an overflow of information, I’ll direct you to this comprehensive resource, and provide a brief explanation: pseudo elements select special parts of some elements. They match based on an English representation of a semantic part of the DOM- for example, :first-letter will select the first element of a paragraph, and :nth-child(x) will select a specific child nodethe argument can also be ‘even’ or ‘odd'.

Tips!

While we’re dabbling with specifics, it’s worth mentioning that there is a world-wide community behind CSS, pushing its development and, well, generally constantly complainingin a friendly way, though!. CSS seems to have a sort of love-it-or-hate-it effect on many people; a polarising topic, as its seeming simplicity is often an entrapment for many a wasted hour fixing breakages (you can still validate your CSS).

Consequently, whole websites have been birthed in the effort to provide ‘hacks’ and ‘tips’ for CSS that make certain tasks easier- the majority of them are ingenious.

Alex Tebbsa seattle-based ui developer is one of many that hail the ‘CSS triangle’ as the “king of CSS hacks”, as we will explore.

I actually used this method in tandem with an ::after pseudoclass to provide the triangles stemming from the tooltipscute, huh? on this website. What you do: create a div that has no height or width- ergo, it is a literal point. Then, add a border on three sides, which fill the spaces and produces a cheaty-arrow! There’s tonnes of techniques such as this, that although not necessarily recommended 100% of the time, can help you grow your understanding and overcome annoying obstacles.
