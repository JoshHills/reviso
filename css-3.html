CSS: Layouts

On this page we will explore how elements are laid out in the page-flow, and how to make them work together without clashing. Fundamental to CSS is the idea that every element is essentially box-like, and when you apply transformations of spacing and positioning, you apply them to one or more inner/outer faces of the box. When using your browser’s developer tools, they may provide a greater insight as to what is creating this invisible space- let’s uncover the mystery theoretically…

Positioning:

It's useful to understand the abstraction of layout when it comes to styling your documents.
You can design for specific contexts- it makes you a better person.

Keeping the thread of thought that all your elements are trapped inside boxes, it’s actually somewhat trivial to re-arrange them. The natural page flow of a document is the source order display of the page’s elements- this tends to render one-after-another. By default, elements are positioned 'relativeas in, stemming from the top-left co-ordinate' to this origin point- by applying top, right, bottom or left, you can essentially push the box in a direction.

By making an element’s position ‘absolute’, you re-anchor it to the top-left of its container, and subsequent specifications of position push it from therethis takes it out of the page flow, hovering it above over content. By making something fixedsuch as the navigation bar for this website, it’s locked to the viewport! Whew, a lot of information to take in, but this topic can be nightmarish without demonstration! There are a few other more context-specific layouts you may explore in your spare time to serve a specific purpose- I encourage you to also peruse the source code of ‘Reviso’ itself to see the diversity in position properties.

Floats:

It works!

No, not the ice-cream variety- floats allow website-content to dynamically reposition itself, at the cost of being incredibly complicated to maintain with a complex structure. Similar to absolutely positioned elements, but these elements essentially form an orderly queue cascading down the page that will move out of the way should the need arise. All of the inline pictures in this website are floated one way or the other to keep things interesting!

Note: If you start using floats and notice that their obfuscating some of your content, you can add ‘clear: both’ to said content to ensure it stays clear of them.

Media Types & Responsive Design:

In the interest of furthering your knowledge of the subject, it’s important that we don’t just get bogged down in syntax- of course, it’s necessary, but it can be stifling at times. On a healthy, positive note, let’s take a look at some design strategies we can follow that will encourage future-proofing, and a generally friendly user experience. A site layout example that adapts to browser viewport width was first demonstrated in 2004- by 2008, terms such as “flexible”, “liquid”, and “fluid” were weaseling their way into the common web-developers idiolect. What’s all the fuss about?

The concept of responsive design is not new. However, increasingly we are being equipped expertly with the tools to implement it, to the extent that not only will it eventually become absolutely vital to a website’s maintenance, but harder to exclude it. Recently, ‘Awwwards’ identified some of the best responsive websites currently being hosted, so you can get a real glimpse at spectacular implementation.

The idea is simple- your service should adapt to the user’s needs, based on contextual hardware factors such as device, screen size/resolution, and interactivity with your medium. The phrase ‘anywhere, everywhere’ springs to mind. The internet is global, so your content should be able to be viewed globally, and in different formats, while still achieving your initial vision.

Jody Resnickceo of trighton interactive, a full-service digital agency that focuses heavily on responsive web design said: “Responsive websites provide continuity between different viewing contexts, remaining completely agnostic to the type of device used and the size of the screen it has. What this means is that the same website will present an optimized layout regardless of which device it finds itself being loaded in.” She then predicts: “leveraging this technology in the future will allow companies to integrate a plethora of back-end services”, which would be both very innovative and very lucrativehey, money makes the world go ‘round.

This is a learned skill that takes time to forge, however you can take the preliminary steps towards this kind of design by starting with it.

Wikipedia, the hub of all holy knowledge, summarises an RWDresponsive web design website as one that adapts the layout to the viewing environment by using fluid, proportion-based grids, flexible images, and CSS3 media queries such as extensions of the ‘@media’ rule.
